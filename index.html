Responsible Methods
Did you know?
Many materials used in AO venue overlays can be reused or recycled. It's how we treat, finish and handle them at bump-out that turns potential resources into waste. Every time we bin a material we throw money away. Here's how to get more out of materials with methods.
Track 1
Design Phase
Design and material choices made upfront determine what's possible at bump-out. Lock these in the brief with builders.
Step 1 — Evergreen Elements
Long-term design (3 year contracts) enable design and build of "evergreen" elements. These reusable assets amplify fan experience building on familiarity year on year. Create a realistic plan to achieve 30% reuse in year 3.
Step 2 — Design Waste Out
Designers have a key role designing waste out. Identify which materials will be reused and which ones will go to a waste stream. Many materials used in AO venue overlays can be reused or recycled. Consider finishes that won't contaminate materials and handling at bump-out such as separating composite materials (e.g. ACM and ply facades).
Step 3 — Circular Design
Modular Design and Design for Disassembly (DfD) create reusable systems. Systems design incorporating connectors — mechanical joinery (bolted, screwed, nailed connections) that don't need chemical bonds (glues, sealers, welding) will allow for disassembly and keep materials recoverable at bump-out.
Step 4 — Choose Materials for reuse
Consult the AO Target Materials List (TML) in design — zone in on whether the same outcome can be delivered that is not single use. The AO TML can tell you what to look for.
Step 5 — Document modular and DfD
Documentation of the system and methods are essential — design plans illustrating how components work together so that future contractors can assemble and dismantle with care.
Step 6 — Evaluate Build Effort & Storage
Work with builders to evaluate which design elements are fit-for-purpose. Consider the effort for a builder to pack down on bump out. Storage logistics and which assets will be retained are considerations in the design stage, not at bump out.
Design-Stage Prompt — Design with the end in mind
"Have I considered design with responsible materials and how to reuse or recover them? Have my methods ensured what we put on materials (paint/graphics) and how we build enables them to be reused or recovered?"

Track 2
Build & Deconstruct Phase
Many materials in waste streams are perfectly reusable and recyclable. It is our methods that turns them into waste. Follow this on pack-down.
Step 1 — Materials matter
Consult the AO Target Materials List (TML) — choose materials that deliver your reuse targets or if not possible to reuse, the material can be recycled. The AO TML can tell you what to look for.
Step 2 — Methods matter
Methods to mitigate waste. Many materials we use today are circular. It is the application of finishes and how they are handled at bump-out that turns them into waste. Mechanical connectors allow disassembly without damage, water-based paint, wood treatments and separation of materials create clean resource streams.
Step 3 — Document modular and DfD
Documentation of the system and methods are essential — using the design illustrations build plans need to document how components work together so that contractors can assemble and dismantle with care.
Step 4 — Disassembly effort
Plan for time and labour needed to pack down and separate waste.
Step 5 — Plan Before You Start
Read the deconstruction (DfD) documentation. Plan effort and resources to pack down. Use the Design for Disassembly instructions, follow the specified steps to avoid damage.
Step 6 — Track & Tag
Record procurement costs and track reused assets — maintain a consistent material tracking log from specification stage through to bump-out and warehouse storage. Tag evergreen assets with circular status, age and condition. This is key in measuring circularity and financial ROI outcomes.
Bump-Out — Planning Prompt
"Have I considered AO target materials and methods to reuse or recover resources? Have I allocated time to disassemble and to label assets with circular status as I pack them for storage?"

Practical ways to reduce waste

Use this guide to help you establish reuse or waste stream up front.

01

Evergreen vs annual

Some temporary event infrastructure components are well suited to be deliberately designed to be reused for a 3–5 year lifecycle with minimal rework, rather than built for a single use. They are modular, durable and brand-agnostic (or easily re-skinned), use mechanical connectors, are documented and tracked so they can be efficiently stored, refurbished, redeployed and ultimately recycled at end of life instead of going to landfill. There are a few types:

i)Foundational: toilets, staff spaces, retail stores, water stations, first aid, counters (back of house / front of house)
ii)Structural: framing / structures / decking
iii)Facades: shade structures / legs, internal hoardings, reusable facades for F&B, marquees and modular structures (Highline / Spacecube)
02

Use AO Target Materials where possible

Many of the materials used today in the AO build are circular — that is, reusable and recyclable. It is how we treat and dispose of materials that creates waste. Finishes and how they are handled on bump-out can keep them out of landfill.

03

Do all materials have a responsible alternative?

Unfortunately some waste is unavoidable because there aren't responsible alternatives and bump-out faces time constraints. The materials that have a substitution challenge are MDF, SAV, outdoor flooring (decking).

i)There are some alternatives for MDF. Some are on par and some priced at a premium. We encourage consideration of the alternatives or if using MDF, commit to modular design so that it can be reused rather than single-use straight to landfill.
ii)Hi-res graphics typically use SAV or ACM (aluminium composite). If they can be separated from substrates some materials can be recovered — especially ACM where aluminium is high value on the secondary market.
iii)Decking has no end-of-life pathway — attempt to design components for reuse.
04

Interventions I can put into practice now

Begin with the end in mind. Consider "what will happen to a material at the end of the event?" It is clear in design and procurement where it will end up — this is the opportunity to design waste out.

Consider the treatments to materials

Use of water-based paint, oils and untreated timbers allows them to be recycled.

For graphics consider the following

•Fabric is an alternative (used in Ballpark AO26)
•High-res graphics bonded to ACM or ply create unrecoverable composites — a high waste category. Aluminium has great resale value and material separation at bump-out keeps it out of landfill.
•Avoid Foamex where possible — many builders consider it inferior and it cannot be recycled easily.

Consider wood products and treatments

There are many types of engineered wood — see our fact sheet. The resins (glues) used in them can send them to landfill. The AO Target List recommends engineered wood and timber products that are suited to reuse and highlights where they can be recycled.

Note: There is extremely limited MDF recycling globally. The formaldehyde resin and fibre composite make it difficult to break down. MDF is the popular choice for joinery / cabinetry / wall flats. The AO request is — take a look at alternatives and consider if the premium is viable over a longer lifecycle, or if it is used, be sure it is in an evergreen element for reuse.
