Responsible Methods
Did you know?
Many materials used in AO venue overlays can be reused or recycled. It's how we treat, finish and handle them at bump-out that turns potential resources into waste. Every time we bin a material we throw money away. Here's how to get more out of materials with methods.
Track 1
Design Phase
Design and material choices made upfront determine what's possible at bump-out. Lock these in the brief with builders.
Evergreen Elements
It makes sense to invest in evergreen elements (long-term design). Identify reusable assets — those that amplify fan experience, building on familiarity year on year. There are 3 categories: (i) Foundational, (ii) Structural and (iii) Facades. As the event planner, TA can enable these through consistent, repeatable park real-estate planning.
Design Waste Out
Designers play a key role in designing waste out. Create a realistic plan to achieve 30% reuse by year 3. Using a reductive approach, first simplify & minimise. Start by considering existing assets in storage that can deliver the intended function. Focus on the essential value, reducing components and streamlining the range of materials. Consider which assets are suited to reuse (evergreen) vs single use (waste).
Modular Design & Design for Disassembly (DfD)
Apply 'systems thinking' — break structures into modules. Modular and DfD processes consider the entire lifecycle of assets, planning for their end of life. Identify assets suited to reusable systems that can be stored. Design for ease of dismantling. Use materials suited to reuse and mechanical joinery (bolted, screwed, nailed connections) rather than chemical bonds (glues, sealers, welding).
Choose Materials for Reuse & Recovery
Zone in on the 80/20. The minority of your material types will make up the majority of your volumes. Incorporate the AO priority materials and keep AO waste-reduction and reuse targets in mind. Use the AO Target Materials List to find suitable materials.
Check AO Recommendation & TA Fire/Safety
The TA Fire/Safety team play a key role in reviewing design intent and materials choice. Be guided by their recommendations in the AO Target Materials List.
Methods Matter — Finishes
Finishes decide the fate of a material. Many materials used in AO venue overlays can be reused or recycled. Choose finishes and bump-out handling that keep materials clean — water-based paints and treatments, and separating composite materials (e.g. ACM and ply facades). This is the moment designers plan which materials will be reused and which ones will go to a waste stream.
Document Modular & DfD
Documentation of the system and methods is essential — create design plans that illustrate how components work together so that future contractors can assemble and dismantle with care.
Evaluate Build Effort & Storage
Work with builders to evaluate which design elements are fit-for-purpose for reuse. Consider the effort for a builder to pack down on bump-out. Storage and logistics are considerations in the design stage, not at bump-out.
Design-Stage Prompt — Design with the end in mind
"Have I optimised evergreen elements with AO target materials as much as possible, and have I planned for how to reuse or recover them? Have my methods ensured what we put on materials (paint/graphics) and how we build enables them to be reused or recovered?"
Track 2
Build & Deconstruct Phase
Many materials in waste streams are perfectly reusable and recyclable. It is our methods that turn them into waste.
Support Delivery of Evergreen Elements
Enable the design intent and build of reusable assets to meet reuse targets. Collaborate with designers on which elements are fit-for-purpose for disassembly and storage.
Document Modular & Design for Disassembly (DfD)
Documentation of the system and methods is essential. Designers may supply DfD documentation or illustrations; builders use these to develop plans showing how components fit together, so contractors can assemble and dismantle with care. Factor in the time and labour needed to pack down and separate materials.
Materials Matter
Consult the AO Target Materials List (TML) — choose materials that deliver your reuse targets or, where reuse isn't possible, ones that can be recycled. The AO Target Materials List shows you what to look for, and the Materials Manager tool keeps a record of materials from procurement through to post-event.
Check AO Recommendation & TA Fire/Safety
The TA Fire/Safety team play a key role in approving materials choice. Use their recommendations in the AO Target Materials List before procurement to speed up approval.
Methods Matter
Methods to mitigate waste. Most materials in use today at the AO are circular. It is the application of finishes and how they are handled at bump-out that turn them into waste. Mechanical connectors allow disassembly without damage; water-based paint, wood treatments and separation of materials create clean resource streams.
Plan Before You Start
Read the deconstruction (DfD) documentation. Plan effort and resources to pack down. Follow the specified disassembly steps to avoid damage.
Keep a Record of Assets — Materials Manager
Record procurement costs and track reused assets — update them in the Materials Manager at bump-out to record each asset's status. Give evergreen assets a circular status and condition. This is key to measuring circularity and financial ROI.
Bump-Out: Every time I bin a material — I throw money away
"Have I used as many AO target materials as possible, guided by TA Fire/Safety recommendations? And have my methods optimised reuse or recovery of resources? Have I allocated time to disassemble and to tag assets for tracking as I pack them for storage?"
Practical ways to reduce waste

Use this guide to help you establish reuse or waste stream up front.

01

Evergreen vs annual

Some temporary event infrastructure components are well suited to be deliberately designed to be reused for a 3–5 year lifecycle with minimal rework, rather than built for a single use. They are modular, durable and brand-agnostic (or easily re-skinned), use mechanical connectors, are documented and tracked so they can be efficiently stored, refurbished, redeployed and ultimately recycled at end of life instead of going to landfill. There are a few types:

i)Foundational: toilets, staff spaces, retail stores, water stations, first aid, counters (back of house / front of house)
ii)Structural: framing / structures / decking
iii)Facades: shade structures / legs, internal hoardings, reusable facades for F&B, marquees and modular structures (Highline / Spacecube)
02

Use the AO target materials and TA Fire/Safety recommendations as much as possible

Many of the materials used today in the AO build are circular — that is, reusable and recyclable. The list provides guidance on what to choose and how to keep them out of landfill. It also includes the TA Fire/Safety advice — use this upfront to speed up approvals when you need it most.

Find the AO Target Materials List here
03

Do all materials have a responsible alternative?

Unfortunately some waste is unavoidable because there aren't responsible alternatives and bump-out faces time constraints. The materials that have a substitution challenge are MDF, SAV, outdoor flooring (decking).

i)There are some alternatives for MDF. Some are on par and some priced at a premium. We encourage consideration of the alternatives or if using MDF, commit to modular design so that it can be reused rather than single-use straight to landfill.
ii)Hi-res graphics typically use SAV or ACM (aluminium composite). If they can be separated from substrates some materials can be recovered — especially ACM where aluminium is high value on the secondary market.
iii)Decking has no end-of-life pathway — attempt to design components for reuse.
04

Interventions I can put into practice now

Begin with the end in mind. Consider "what will happen to a material at the end of the event?" It is clear in design and procurement where it will end up — this is the opportunity to design waste out.

Consider the treatments to materials

Use of water-based paint, oils and untreated timbers allows them to be recycled.

For graphics consider the following

•Fabric is an alternative (used in Ballpark AO26)
•High-res graphics bonded to ACM or ply create unrecoverable composites — a high waste category. Aluminium has great resale value and material separation at bump-out keeps it out of landfill.
•Avoid Foamex where possible — many builders consider it inferior and it cannot be recycled easily.

Consider wood products and treatments

There are many types of engineered wood — see our fact sheet. The resins (glues) used in them can send them to landfill. The AO Target List recommends engineered wood and timber products that are suited to reuse and highlights where they can be recycled.

Note: There is extremely limited MDF recycling globally. The formaldehyde resin and fibre composite make it difficult to break down. MDF is the popular choice for joinery / cabinetry / wall flats. The AO request is — take a look at alternatives and consider if the premium is viable over a longer lifecycle, or if it is used, be sure it is in an evergreen element for reuse.
